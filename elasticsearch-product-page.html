Step 1. The Elasticsearch page: what’s wrong

UX found some problems. SEO found others. This table shows where they agree, where only one source speaks, and what is still missing.

How to read this: UX covers the product story, persuasion, and developer experience. SEO covers documented search and page-template findings. When the SEO audit did not comment on a section, the table says so.
Section, top to bottom UX problem SEO problem Read
01Hero The hero is a list of features, not a promise. A developer feels nothing and has no clear reason to keep scrolling. Documented: the H1 does not lead with a searchable keyword. The hero visual is generic instead of showing real product UI. AgreeThe hero fails people and search engines. It does not create desire, explain the category, or make the product feel real.
02Right under the hero The page never names the problem. It does not explain why a raw AI model is not enough. Documented: the problem section is missing. The template calls for 3 to 4 sourced pain statistics directly after the hero. AgreeBoth findings want a problem section here. The current page has none.
03Use cases The page shows simple use cases where a simpler tool may honestly be easier for developers. Elastic is strongest when the job also needs permissions, live data, business rules, exact and semantic search together, or private deployment. The page competes where it can be beaten and stays quiet where it is hardest to replace. No documented finding on these specific cards. The SEO template gives a general format for capability sections, but the audit does not judge this strategy. UX-ledThis is a product-positioning problem, not a page-format problem.
04The four product cards The page splits Elasticsearch into datastore, vector database, search engine, and geospatial cards. That creates four separate feature fights. Developers can find a stronger single-purpose tool on any one card. Elastic’s real advantage is that all four work together in one system, and the page hides that advantage. No documented finding. The card shape is similar to the SEO deep-dive format, but the audit does not flag these cards. UX-ledThe page invites the feature-by-feature comparison Elastic should avoid.
05Proof The page makes claims without evidence. It also ignores Elastic’s hardest advantage to copy: years in production, flexible deployment, security at the core, and many data types working in one system. Documented: the sourced proof-stat bar is missing. Competitors name studies and reports. This page does not provide comparable sources. AgreeThe page makes strong claims but does not prove them.
06Customer proof The page shows logos and short, general statements. There is no named person, clear story, or measurable result. The review widget feels generic. Documented: customer proof needs a named person, title, company, quote, outcome metric, and case-study link. The audit describes the current state as logos only. AgreeThis is the cleanest match. Vague logos do not help a buyer understand what changed for a real customer.
07Getting started and smart search These modules float in the middle of the page without a clear story around them. They ask people to build before the page has earned belief. No documented finding on these modules. UX-ledThe issue is timing and persuasion, not an SEO requirement.
08What is Elasticsearch? The page assumes visitors already understand Elasticsearch and jumps directly to features. Many visitors may leave because they never understood the product, not because they rejected it. Documented: “What is Elasticsearch?” is an important query and a format AI assistants often quote. The page does not answer it clearly, so other sources can win the answer. AgreeBoth findings point to the same basic gap: the page never clearly explains what Elasticsearch is.
09The developer is never the hero Almost every sentence is about Elastic. The page rarely shows what the developer can build, improve, or become. No documented finding. The SEO audit does not measure voice or who the story makes important. UX-ledThis is a voice and persuasion problem.
10Missing modules UX did not produce one combined checklist for these modules. The broader issue is that late-stage buyers do not get enough evidence, comparison, or help making a decision. MissingSourced proof-stat bar, analyst recognition cards, ROI calculator, competitive comparison, and FAQ schema. Several pages also end with only one CTA instead of two. SEO-ledThese are documented, measurable gaps in the solution-page template.
Step 1 of 5. The strongest overlap is clear: the page needs a better promise, a real problem, proof, credible customer evidence, and a simple explanation of what Elasticsearch is.

Section and content annotations

Hero

Job: Sell the customer’s ambition, not a list of Elasticsearch features.

Write: A 6 to 10 word H1, a 25 to 40 word subhead, and two CTAs. Lead with “Build experiences only your business can create.” Keep the layout centered and text only.

What’s new

Job: Show that the product is moving forward without interrupting the main story.

Write: A small heading and three update cards. Each card needs a short title, one clear sentence, an availability label, and a link. Roadmap names need public approval before publishing.

Trust

Job: Make the hero promise believable.

Show: Recognizable customer logos only. Use companies that run important search or AI experiences in production.

Core thesis

Job: Explain why the same AI model does not create the same product.

Write: One centered opening paragraph, then three short cards: Data brings business knowledge together, Context decides what matters, and Agentic AI turns that understanding into answers and action.

Data, layer 1

Heading direction: Every dimension of your data, working as one.

Three parts: Explain structured data, text, vectors, geography, relationships, and live signals. Prove real-time performance, scale, open APIs, resilience, and deployment choice. Demonstrate one experience using several data types together.

Context, layer 2

Heading direction: Make your definition of “best” the advantage.

Three parts: Explain retrieval relevance and business relevance. Prove that search, reranking, rules, permissions, freshness, and source authority work together. Demonstrate a request becoming a ranked, secure context package.

Agentic AI, layer 3

Heading direction: Build agents that do more than talk.

Three parts: Explain grounded answers and multi-step action. Prove the agent stays current, secure, and model independent. Demonstrate an agent retrieving, checking rules, using a tool, and completing or approving a workflow.

Validation

Job: Show that the full Data, Context, and Agentic AI story works in production.

Include: Three sourced metrics, one named customer story with a measurable outcome, and two analyst or independent review cards. Every number needs a source.

Getting started

Heading direction: One platform from first prototype to your best product.

Three parts: Connect your data. Build and test retrieval or agents. Deploy, measure, and improve. Link each card to useful documentation, code, or a hands-on starting point.

FAQ

Job: Remove the last technical and buying questions.

Write: Five to seven direct Q&As. Cover LLMs versus search, vector database use, mixed data types, relevance control, permissions, model choice, and deployment. Add FAQPage schema.
